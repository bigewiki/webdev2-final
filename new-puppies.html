New Puppies

Puppies are a challenge

It’s a great idea to be prepared for your new furry family member to come home. Here are some of the basic supplies you might want to get before or immediately after bringing in your pup.

Puppy Food

If possible, find out what brand of food your puppy was eating before you take them home. In our case, we were given a small bag of food to take along with our pup. Assuming you aren’t sticking with whatever kind of food your pup was eating before you’ll want to research some puppy food brands and varieties. You may not stick with the new food for a while but it’s ideal if you do. All dogs need to be gradually transitioned to a new kind of food over time. This conversion should take about a week but a more steady transition is preferred, over the course of two weeks. Every day increase the proportion of new food in your dog’s bowl, a ten percent incremental increase is reasonable here.

Treats

When you start training it’s necessary to have treats on hand. I recommend using the tiny training treats, your pup will love them! There are alternatives to treats for training, even kibbles of puppy food are acceptable, but being prepared with treats lets you take advantage of more training opportunities with your pup.

Bowls

Among the must-haves are bowls, one for water and one for food, of course. No need to go overboard with the supplies, you could simply borrow an old dish or Tupperware for the short term. Avoid anything breakable, such as ceramic or glass.

Toys

You’ll want to have a few different kinds of toys to captivate your puppy’s attention. Pup’s love to tear apart the softer, plushier toys. This doesn’t mean you should avoid them, just be wary of whatever’s stuffed inside: your pup may want to eat the stuffing or that stuffing may end up all over the place. Have tougher toys on hand, anything that can take a lot of punishment will last a long time, especially as your puppy starts teething. Ropes are great for playing tug-of-war with your dog, a beloved canine pastime. Toys that engage your dog’s mind, such as puzzle toys, are awesome too. Do your research here and be mindful of what’s appropriate for your dog’s age and size.

Crate, Playpen, and Baby Gates

I’ll cover the value of these items in greater details in the boundaries section below. These could be the most expensive of the pet supplies you need. I recommend having, at the very least, a kennel for your pup. You may need to upgrade the size over time but most new kennels come with an extra partition you can use to section off part of the kennel, which you can remove as your pup grows into it.

Baby gates range in price and quality. There may not be much reason in spending too much money on this as it’s only really needed for the short term unless you have an infant.

Puppy playpens are great for when you want to give your canine a bit more space but can’t keep a vigilant watch over them while you’re busy. When shopping for a puppy playpen be mindful of your pup’s size, breed, and how quickly they’ll grow.

All these items are great to find second hand at a discount, just be sure to properly clean and disinfect for the health and safety of you and your puppy.

Bed and Blankets

Dogs love having somewhere soft to rest, this could end up being your bed or sofa. As dog moms and dads all observe different policies when it comes to our dogs and our furniture, while your pup is still being potty trained you may want to discuss and come to a family agreement on what this policy is. Regardless, every dog should have a bed of some sort, for those on a budget an old blanket will do great. You may not want to invest too much money on a dog bed as your pup is likely to chew on it. It’s only natural and should be expected.

Collar, Harness, and Leash

Every domestic dog should, of course, have a collar. This may not be an immediate necessity but it provides a bit of peace of mind when you’ve attached a dog tag that helps him or her find their way home if they manage to escape. Even for puppies who are too young to leave the house, having a leash and harness can be great for training and maintaining control of your puppy and their environment. It’s not a bad idea to introduce a leash and harness to your pup early to foster familiarity with the concept and make leash training on walks easier in the future. I cover this a bit more in the boundaries section below.

Other Supplies

Your puppy is likely to need a bath every so often, be sure to pick up a safe and appropriate shampoo. Do yourself a favor and be prepared with something to clean pet messes from carpets and floors. Oral hygiene is too often neglected, your dog should have regular teeth cleanings. It’s a good idea to have the supplies for brushing your canine’s teeth and making this a regular ritual. I won’t go into detail here but I encourage you to do some research into doggy oral health and become better informed on the topic.

Life Hacks

There’s a ton of ways to save money on pet supplies. Consider getting some of these items second hand, other household’s pets frequently outgrow or no longer need some of the things you may need for your puppy. Also, you could check out some other clever solutions other pet owners have come up with, check out this video, and hit Google, Pinterest, YouTube, etcetera, for more ideas.

Puppy Health

Talk To Your Vet

Your puppy will need several shots and vaccinations during their first months, years, and regularly throughout their lifetime. Always consult your vet on what your pup needs to be happy and healthy. Your vet should recommend keeping your dog home while they’re very young to avoid microbial threats to their wellbeing. Check out this guide from the American Kennel Club for more information.

Consider spaying or neutering your dog when they’re old enough. I recommend doing some research on the subject if you’re on the fence. There are valid benefits to consider for the health, safety, and behavior of your dog.

Building Trust

First Things First

Before diving into heavy and focused training sessions with your dog take the time to form a bond and connect with your new family member. They will respond much better to training if they trust you, in fact, your dog will respond better to you overall and be more cooperative if you have that strong level of trust. As your puppy is young it will form behaviors and reactions to people and situations based on how you handle them. It’s very easy to get angry or frustrated at your dog but it does more harm than good to act on impulse. Avoid yelling and martial punishment and do your research on how to properly assert yourself and what methods of discipline might work. In my experience, positive reinforcement is more powerful and almost eliminates the need for punishment.

Boundaries

Keep Out of Reach

With a new puppy you should be prepared to puppy-proof any space they’ll hang out in. Puppies love to explore, chew, and make messes, this is to be expected and can’t really be avoided. If they can get to it, they’ll find it. You can mitigate the damage and redirect their attention to what they are allowed to do by protecting, moving, and/or removing the following:

  • Cords and cables
  • Trash bins
  • House plants
  • Chemicals/cleaning solutions
  • Bags of dog food
  • etc.
Giving your pup too much freedom early on is generally a bad idea. Over time, as they earn your trust, you can ease the limitations you place.

Furniture

Some pet owners forbid their dogs from jumping on sofas and beds, others invite the opportunity to cuddle with their furry companions. Discuss a long term policy with the stakeholders in your household and get everyone’s agreement on what rules will be observed. It’s important that everyone is consistent with the policy. Your pup might be allowed more freedom as they earn more trust over time, which should also be agreed upon for consistency’s sake.

Be aware of the risk with puppies to soil and chew your furniture. Limiting their access and having constant supervision while there’s a risk to your furniture is certainly advised as replacing furniture isn’t cheap. Also, be aware that pups love to crawl behind and underneath your furniture.

Controlled Environment

Your puppy will require constant supervision when they’re outside of their crate. You can make this requirement a lot less stressful by putting up baby gates or other barriers, such as closing doors, to parts of the house where your puppy can get into trouble. Establish these barriers first thing so your pup doesn’t become confused by any sort of reduction in their play area, they’re more likely to test a barrier in this case and figure out how to circumvent it. This is very important for potty training as well since your pup will go somewhere less obvious to relieve themselves.

A great idea for giving your pup a bit more freedom than their kennel allows but still keeping them out of trouble is having a puppy playpen. This shouldn’t replace a kennel since they might still be prone to have accidents inside their playpen and might even figure out a way to escape. Nevertheless, a puppy playpen can be great for when you’re busy doing something nearby, such as working, exercising, or cooking. Be mindful of your puppy’s size and breed when selecting a playpen, make sure it’s tall enough and introduce them to it early on before they get too used to having too much freedom.

My puppy very easily scaled the walls of her playpen, the walls were too short and had horizontal pieces she could use as rungs to climb over. Early on she would knock the whole thing down as she put her weight against one side. I fashioned a set of 2x4s together to make a base for stability, assuming that knocking it over was her only means of escape. The solution worked great for helping the playpen keep its shape but she could still climb over, using the 2x4s as an extra, very solid step. I ultimately returned it and dismantled my 2x4 rig, but a puppy playpen could still work for you.

Another trick for maintaining control of your pup’s environment is by using a leash and harness indoors. You can use the leash to keep your pup close to you where they’re less likely to get into trouble. Even if you don’t attach the leash to you this can be a helpful aid for training and getting your pup out from under and/or behind furniture and other hard-to-reach places your pup can squeeze into. Your puppy will likely chew on their leash, especially if they’re not used to it. This should be expected, you and your pup can work on breaking this habit over time.

Crate Training

Consider a Crate

It may seem cruel and inhumane to crate your pup, they’ll whine and whimper at first, which doesn’t help with this misconception. Crate training can be a powerful supplement to potty training and keep your pup out of trouble and protect your property. Done properly, your puppy’s crate can become a home for them, a safe place to rest and relax. Ultimately, it’s up to you, the pet owner, on whether you should use a crate. I’ll make a strong case in favor of crate training that might change your mind to give it a try. Crates may not be for every dog.

Limits

First and foremost: limits. A general rule of thumb is that you can keep your pup in a kennel for one hour for each month of age they’ve gotten under their belt (or collar, in the case of a dog). A two-month-old puppy cannot stay in a kennel for longer than two hours, a three-month-old puppy cannot stay in a kennel for longer than three hours, and so on. The upper limit here is up to five or six hours for older puppies, which should be avoided. Make arrangements for you or someone you trust to let your pup out while you’re working or away for extended periods. Make sure your crate is appropriately sized for your dog. Often, crates come with partitions to make a smaller section for your puppy, which is helpful for potty training and gives them space to grow into it when the partition is removed. If your dog is cramped in their crate upgrade it to something larger and more accommodating to their size.

Foundation

You should be slow and methodical about introducing your pup to their crate. Your puppy will learn to accept their crate as a safe refuge over time if crate training is done correctly. Eventually, they will choose to go in and out their crate willingly, with the door wide open. Help foster, within your pup, a positive association with their crate with a bit of training and motivation. Train them to come in and out of their crate and reward them for the effort. When first closing the door on them, show them the door opens again, they aren’t trapped in there forever. Increase the duration as gradually as you can. Make it cozy, get a crate mat or some blankets. While they’re inside, give them a toy and/or a bone to keep them occupied. Also, never use the crate as punishment.

Exercise

Make sure your pup has an opportunity to burn some energy before they go into their crate. If you’re giving your puppy a break during an extended stay in their crate and plan to put them back in, they will need some activity during this time as well as being allowed some time to do their outside business. Exercise, overall, helps reduce anxiety and makes for a better behaved and more cooperative dog. Fetch and tug-of-war are great ways for your puppy to burn some energy and play at the same time.

Potty Training

As previously mentioned, and covered in more detail in our potty training section, crates are a perfect supplement to potty training. Because crates are a small area where your puppy stays for extended periods they will learn not to use this space to relieve themselves and will become conditioned to hold it until they can go outside. Consider getting a large crate your pup can grow into and comes with a separator you can use to section off part of the crate. You can remove this partition once your pup is bigger and less likely to have accidents inside their crate.

Night Time

Since puppies are prone to have accidents you may not want to let your puppy sleep in your bed, at least not while they are very young. They could also fall off, which could hurt them or be a very unpleasant experience. When it’s bedtime move the crate inside your bedroom, if needed, and position it so you and your pup have a clear view of each other. This is very reassuring to your puppy, especially for those first few nights. Place the kennel somewhere higher off the ground if possible so they can see you better, at least while your puppy gets used to sleeping in his/her crate. Expect to hear a lot of whining, particularly at first. Be understanding that this is a new and strange experience for them and rest assured that it will get better over time. Expect, with your new puppy, and while kennel training, to be woken up several times at night. When your puppy starts getting a handle of potty training these midnight whimpers, whines, and barks could be your pup’s way of letting you know that they need to go outside.

Puppy Biting

Why Does My Puppy Bite?

Puppy biting is easily one of the most annoying and difficult habits to break from. Those tiny teeth hurt and can cause some damage. This is especially problematic with small children, always stay close and vigilant when kids and puppies are playing together.

Some puppies are worse about this habit than others, but it should be understood that this is a natural puppy instinct. They use their mouths to explore the world and need to learn how to control their strength through experience. Deterring puppy biting, especially with aggressive and rowdy pups, takes time and patience but should be addressed with priority.

What To Do

Some trainers recommend grabbing a puppy’s ear or collar when they bite, or even biting back. Your training method is at your discretion but I highly discourage being these practices as they can violate trust and have lasting negative effects.

Instead, use chew toys to show your puppy what is okay to bite and chew on. Distract them with a game of tug of war and show them how to play in a more acceptable way. Over time your puppy will start to get it, the biting will be replaced by licking, which is definitely preferred! Puppies may not understand ‘no’ or ‘ouch’ but these words can start to gain meaning.

Keep in mind that your puppy has a lot of energy, they want to play, and to them, biting is playing. Stay a step ahead, as you start encountering this issue you’ll start to notice when your puppy is most likely to bite. Be prepared with a toy in hand, captivate their attention in a healthier way.

Treats are a great way to change modes when your puppy is in the mood to nip. There’s a lot of raw energy that could be harnessed into a more productive training session. Ask your pup to do something else that you’ve taught them or teach them something new, it’s a great distraction.

Calm Down

Another powerful way to stop puppy biting is to get your puppy to settle down a little bit. Place them in a boring environment and stop engaging. Avoid making this place their kennel as their kennel should have positive associations with your pup. Be sure to keep calm yourself, it’s very easy to become angry and frustrated at this aggressive behavior, I know I did. Take some time to look into other techniques for calming your puppy down. Remember, this is only a phase and it will pass with the proper training.
