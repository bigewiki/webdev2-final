New Puppies

Puppies are a challenge

It’s a great idea to be prepared for your new furry family member to come home. Here are some of the basic supplies you might want to get before or immediately after bringing in your pup.

Puppy Food

If possible, find out what brand of food your puppy was eating before you take them home. In our case, we were given a small bag of food to take along with our pup. Assuming you aren’t sticking with whatever kind of food your pup was eating before you’ll want to research some puppy food brands and varieties. You may not stick with the new food for a while but it’s ideal if you do. All dogs need to be gradually transitioned to a new kind of food over time. This conversion should take about a week but a more steady transition is preferred, over the course of two weeks. Every day increase the proportion of new food in your dog’s bowl, a ten percent incremental increase is reasonable here.

Treats

When you start training it’s necessary to have treats on hand. I recommend using the tiny training treats, your pup will love them! There are alternatives to treats for training, even kibbles of puppy food are acceptable, but being prepared with treats lets you take advantage of more training opportunities with your pup.

Bowls

Among the must-haves are bowls, one for water and one for food, of course. No need to go overboard with the supplies, you could simply borrow an old dish or Tupperware for the short term. Avoid anything breakable, such as ceramic or glass.

Toys

You’ll want to have a few different kinds of toys to captivate your puppy’s attention. Pup’s love to tear apart the softer, plushier toys. This doesn’t mean you should avoid them, just be wary of whatever’s stuffed inside: your pup may want to eat the stuffing or that stuffing may end up all over the place. Have tougher toys on hand, anything that can take a lot of punishment will last a long time, especially as your puppy starts teething. Ropes are great for playing tug-of-war with your dog, a beloved canine pastime. Toys that engage your dog’s mind, such as puzzle toys, are awesome too. Do your research here and be mindful of what’s appropriate for your dog’s age and size.

Crate, Playpen, and Baby Gates

I’ll cover the value of these items in greater details in the (((BOUNDARIES LINK))) section below. These could be the most expensive of the pet supplies you need. I recommend having, at the very least, a kennel for your pup. You may need to upgrade the size over time but most new kennels come with an extra partition you can use to section off part of the kennel, which you can remove as your pup grows into it.

Baby gates range in price and quality. There may not be much reason in spending too much money on this as it’s only really needed for the short term unless you have an infant.

Puppy playpens are great for when you want to give your canine a bit more space but can’t keep a vigilant watch over them while you’re busy. When shopping for a puppy playpen be mindful of your pup’s size, breed, and how quickly they’ll grow.

All these items are great to find second hand at a discount, just be sure to properly clean and disinfect for the health and safety of you and your puppy.

Bed and Blankets

Dogs love having somewhere soft to rest, this could end up being your bed or sofa. As dog moms and dads all observe different policies when it comes to our dogs and our furniture, while your pup is still being potty trained you may want to discuss and come to a family agreement on what this policy is. Regardless, every dog should have a bed of some sort, for those on a budget an old blanket will do great. You may not want to invest too much money on a dog bed as your pup is likely to chew on it. It’s only natural and should be expected.

Collar, Harness, and Leash

Every domestic dog should, of course, have a collar. This may not be an immediate necessity but it provides a bit of peace of mind when you’ve attached a dog tag that helps him or her find their way home if they manage to escape. Even for puppies who are too young to leave the house, having a leash and harness can be great for training and maintaining control of your puppy and their environment. It’s not a bad idea to introduce a leash and harness to your pup early to foster familiarity with the concept and make leash training on walks easier in the future. I cover this a bit more in the (((BOUNDARIES LINK))) section below.

Other Supplies

Your puppy is likely to need a bath every so often, be sure to pick up a safe and appropriate shampoo. Do yourself a favor and be prepared with something to clean pet messes from carpets and floors. Oral hygiene is too often neglected, your dog should have regular teeth cleanings. It’s a good idea to have the supplies for brushing your canine’s teeth and making this a regular ritual. I won’t go into detail here but I encourage you to do some research into doggy oral health and become better informed on the topic.

Life Hacks

There’s a ton of ways to save money on pet supplies. Consider getting some of these items second hand, other household’s pets frequently outgrow or no longer need some of the things you may need for your puppy. Also, you could check out some other clever solutions other pet owners have come up with, check out this video, and hit Google, Pinterest, YouTube, etcetera, for more ideas.

Puppy Health

Your puppy will need several shots and vaccinations during their first months, years, and regularly throughout their lifetime. Always consult your vet on what your pup needs to be happy and healthy. Your vet should recommend keeping your dog home while they’re very young to avoid microbial threats to their wellbeing. Check out this guide from the American Kennel Club (((LINK))) for more information.

https://www.akc.org/expert-advice/health/puppy-shots-complete-guide/

Consider spaying or neutering your dog when they’re old enough. I recommend doing some research on the subject if you’re on the fence. There are valid benefits to consider for the health, safety, and behavior of your dog.
