Training

Where to start?

You should start with small bits of training with your new puppy right away, but remember, you can train your dog at any age, even older dogs can benefit from training. It’s likely you want to start with the fundamentals, the most important things you can teach your dog are in this category. These basics include potty training, come (recall), sit, and stay. I cover those in more detail below.

With new puppies the primary thing you should focus on is building trust but take advantage of training opportunities at the same time.

  • Try to keep an eye on the cues from your dog on their readiness to tackle a new challenge.
  • Be consistent with your training and the rules, follow through every time.
  • Positive reinforcement is more powerful than punishment.
  • Play with your dog often to fortify trust and bonding.
  • Be sure to nurture your dog’s trust in you.
  • Keep training, bite-size treats on hand, always. Be generous with the treats but make sure your dog earns them!
  • If your dog seems to be losing interest or is distracted, that may be a sign to take a break and come back to the session later.
  • Be patient with your dog, avoid getting frustrated and angry, it’s detrimental to focus that anger or frustration at your dog.

Potty Training

Disclaimer

We should start this segment with a disclaimer: There will be accidents, if you have carpet your puppy will soil it. Along with all the other supplies you need, pick up some sort of carpet cleaner, and test it on a small section of your carpet that will go unnoticed. Don’t be caught unprepared here. An old belief with handling accidents to yell at them or rub their nose in it, which should not be practiced as this damages trust and is likely to create other problems. Expressing disapproval in a healthy way is okay, a stern ‘no’ is appropriate on the spot, but not when you find the mess later. If you catch them in the middle of conducting their business indoors take them outside immediately to demonstrate where it’s okay. Positive reinforcement is a lot more powerful here, rewarding your puppy every time they relieve themselves in the proper place, and over time they’ll have fewer accidents. Be patient, this process can be frustrating and even take months, but done properly you will see progress.

Routine

Routine is very key to potty training your puppy. You might start to notice a pattern with the timing of accidents you find in the house, especially in relation to when they eat. For this reason, mealtime should happen on a consistent schedule. Your puppy might also drop hints when they need to do their business and is likely to favor the same indoor spots to have their accidents. Try to take your puppy out frequently, especially first thing in the morning, last thing at night, and after every meal. Very young puppies might need to go in the middle of the night, expect disruption to your sleep for the first couple months as an investment to long term success with potty training.

Crate Training

Kennel training is covered in more depth here and is key to the potty training effort. Be sure to check out that section about kennel training before introducing your dog to their crate. Dogs will avoid soiling the place they sleep as they become more accustomed to their kennel. A rule of thumb is to limit their stay in the kennel to one hour for every month of age. For example, a two-month-old puppy should spend no longer than 2 hours in the kennel, a three-month-old puppy is 3 hours. The top limit is about five to six hours for older pups but it’s highly discouraged to keep any poor dog kenneled for that long if it can be avoided. If you work during the day arrange to have someone come and let your pup out once or twice while you’re away.

Controlled Environment

I talk more about controlled environments here, but this is very important to minimizing accidents and keeping an eye on your pup at all times. Constant supervision is required for your young puppy and if you can’t watch him/her ask someone else to keep an eye on them while you’re busy. A short stay in the kennel is acceptable, or, if you have one, a puppy playpen is great too. Your young puppy should not be allowed to wander the house, I recommend baby gates and closing doors. Dogs will favor somewhere out of your view to have their accidents, eliminate this risk by blocking access to these areas. Alternatively, you could use a leash and harness to keep your pup close to you, even in the house. This is a great opportunity to introduce leash training and keep your dog close to you. I don’t have any content on leash training but I recommend Zak George’s training videos, you’ll find them all over my site.

Come (Recall)

The basics

Training your dog to come when called, otherwise known as recall, is one of the most important things you can teach your dog. This isn’t only important for having a dog who listens to you and stays out of trouble but also for their own safety. The sooner you can start training your dog recall, the better. It’s important to consistently reward them when they come, no matter how long it takes. The need for rewarding can be eased after a while but it’s very important to condition the belief that if they come when they are called good things always happen. For this same reason, you should never call your dog to come to you for punishment. Early on, set aside time to have training sessions to introduce the ‘come’ command and reward liberally. Take baby steps and ease the difficulty slowly over time. Take a break if your pup starts losing interest.

Controlled Environment

Distractions can derail your dog’s attention and create a frustrating experience for you when your dog doesn’t listen. Early on, start in a small space with minimal distraction. Of course, this doesn’t prepare your dog for the real world, but when you’re starting out you want to start small and increase the difficulty from there. Many dog trainers use leashes indoors to make sure their pups stay close and are always accessible, for their own safety. Pups love to crawl under beds and behind furniture, they may not want to come out when called. I don’t recommend using the leash to pull your dog to motivate them to come when called unless you’re pulling them out of somewhere they shouldn’t be. Let them learn and ease them into it by making it easy at first. Eventually, your dog will want to come to you when you ask them to.

Make It Fun

Get your puppy excited to come to you! This helps keep them engaged and gives you both some enjoyment for any training session. Use a higher-pitched and inviting tone to motivate them to listen and let them know how good they’re doing. Especially with puppies, but also with older dogs, get on your feet, lead them to chase you, incorporate some fun movement. Toys, especially squeaky ones, are awesome at getting your dog’s attention and adding some enjoyment to the training session. Note that, sometimes, you may need to get down on the floor or crouch down a bit, it helps to get on their level and let them see your face.

Use Your Tools

It goes without saying that treats are important to any training effort, make sure it’s a treat that your pup loves. Try to keep the treats on you or nearby, countless training opportunities will present themself throughout the day and only take a second to take advantage of. As previously mentioned, toys and a leash are great tools to make this easier but don’t pull the leash when they aren’t listening if it’s not necessary. Dogs also respond well to nonverbal cues or gestures such as pointing your dog to where you want them to be. Some trainers like to use clickers as an indication to a dog that they like a specific behavior. With the ‘come’ command I found finger-snapping to be something my dogs respond well to and a great indicator of where I want them to come to.

Sit

Foundations

It’s not reasonable to expect your dog to understand what they should do when you command a sit, you’ll need to start slow and level up the difficulty at a slow and gradual pace. An easy win is to get your puppy's attention on a treat and ease them into a sit by, very, very slowly, lifting the treat above their head. A natural tendency here is for your dog to sit so they stabilize themselves to look up and try and get the treat. Your pup might try standing on two feet to get a higher stance, this is the opposite of what you want. Keep working on it, release the treat as soon as their butt hits the ground.

A hand gesture is very helpful here too, just the simple act of lifting up the treat helps make the request easier to understand in the early stages of teaching your dog to sit. I use a gesture of holding out my hand and bending my wrist back as the visual cue for my dog to sit down. Over time you can start to phase the gesture so your dog starts responding to the verbal command alone.

Note that, at this point, we haven’t introduced the sit command just yet. It’s okay to repeat the word sit in order to build that association with the command and the action, but don’t expect an immediate response early on. Once your dog starts catching on that they get a treat each time they sit they’ll do it without prompt, and this is okay. Reward liberally early on and start increasing the difficulty. With time and patience, your canine will plop their bottom down when you ask them too.

Paying Attention

A challenge you’ll notice any dog is their tendency to become distracted and stop paying attention to you. This can derail any training session, so it’s important we start out in a quiet environment. It’s worth investing time in getting your dog to simply acknowledge their name. A training exercise of its own is to reward your dog to look up at you when you say their name. Getting them to watch your hands is good too, which they’ll do anyway because they are interested in the treat you’re holding. This is a great opportunity to use gestures, which your pup might more easily notice.

Long Term

The sit command is easy to reinforce over time. Don’t expect your pup to understand what they should do in your second and third training sessions even if they had it down the time before. It takes practice to build a habit. You may have to ask them multiple times early on, don’t be discouraged. Keep treats on hand and give them a surprise request to sit outside of a training session and in a more distracting environment. Eventually, you can ease off the liberal reward but give it a long while before you start doing so. You may be tempted to ease the frequency of rewards when your dog starts becoming pretty dependable with any given behavior, but this may invite regression in the progress you and your pup have made in any area of training.

Stay

Foundations

The stay command is a natural transition from sit, they’re already halfway there if they’ve got this down. Reward liberally early on, it’s difficult for a puppy to hold still and to understand you want them to stay put. Adding the additional challenge of making them wait for the treat while sitting, when they’re ready, makes adding stay an easy command to introduce. Be careful about expecting your pup to stay for too long, too early. Very gradually start adding time, it’s very easy to tell when you’ve asked for too much.

Let your pup know when the ‘stay’ is over, invite them to come to get the treat or hand it over. Patience and encouragement are key, just like with any other training exercise. I find a gesture to be helpful here, my chosen gesture is to point at my dog while saying stay. Eventually, if you wish, you can phase out the gesture but it’s great to use when your puppy isn’t getting it with the verbal cue alone.

Distractions

Of course, early on, you’ll want to start in a quiet area where your pup is more likely to continue paying attention to you. This isn’t always a realistic scenario so as you become more confident in your dog start introducing some distractions. Puppies are easily distracted by movement, especially if it’s quick. If you threw a treat your dog is likely to chase it. Make it easy at first, move the treat slowly in front of them, then let them have it. You can start increasing the speed and distance of this movement over time. Try setting the treat down for a split second, then leave that little temptation out for a bit longer over several iterations.
